The fake data project

Real charts. Real methodology. Entirely made-up numbers.

Some things are easy to measure. Box office grosses, election margins, rainfall. But most of the things people actually argue about have no dataset behind them. How relevant was democracy in 1994? How much did the world care about cultural history in 2007? Nobody was counting. There is nothing to cite.

This site fills that gap with confidence, if not with facts. For every topic in the archive, we ask a large language model to rate the topic’s relevance for each year from 1980 to 2020, on a scale from 0 to 1. The model has read a meaningful fraction of the internet and will answer almost any question with a straight face. We take its numbers and draw the kind of clean, authoritative charts that usually imply somebody did actual research.

About the asterisk: the sparkle that normally means “an AI made this” is doing the job of the footnote that means “see disclaimer.” Every figure here is fake data — statistics-shaped output from a model that was never asked to show its work. The asterisk is load-bearing.

How it works

  1. A topic enters the archive.
  2. A language model scores the topic’s relevance, year by year, from 0 (nobody cared) to 1 (inescapable).
  3. We chart it, sparkline it, and compare its trend against other topics’.
  4. No humans fact-check anything. That would defeat the purpose.

Should you cite this data? No. Should you make decisions with it? Absolutely not. But if one of these charts looks plausible enough to make you squint, that’s the interesting part. Plausibility is the one thing language models manufacture at scale — and it looks a lot like data.
